It is using its operating-system-based gorilla power to continually push Netscape off balance. Every time it succeeds, a few of the other members in the marketplace shift their allegiance a little bit, and Microsoft gains a bit of additional market share, which increases its competitive advantage a little bit more.
This sort of attack is extremely difficult to combat. It is part of a larger economic pattern that has been explained by Brian Arthur and others at the Santa Fe Institute as the law of increasing returns. Unlike traditional markets, where the power of the market leader is contained at the point of diminishing returns, in technology-based markets there is no diminishing return, and therefore no containment. The powerful simply get more and more powerful. This effect is at the heart of the Department of Justice's heartburn over Microsoft, Intel, and others; for by all preceding rules of competition, the only way one could acquire the kind of power they have is through illegal monopolistic practices. In reality, however, what we are seeing is the essential gorilla advantage, the basis for our claim that, in any technology market, the investor's desired end state is to be 100% invested in the gorilla. Gorilla power may be unfair, but it is not illegal, and it is a stockholder's joy.
